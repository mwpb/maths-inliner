Trigonometry Definitions

The word sine is derived from the Sanskrit word jya meaning bowstring.

In the picture on the right the bow is marked in black and the bowstring is in green. The circle has radius equal to $1$.

Move the red point a little to see how the bow and bowstring get bigger and smaller as you change the angle a.

Today we use $sin(a)$ to denote the length in green that is half of a bowstring.

The word secant is derived from the Latin word secare meaning to cut. It is the line that is 'cut' by the angle $a$. Move the red point to see how changing the angle cuts out a different line.

The word tangent is derived from the Latin word tangere meaning to touch. It is the line 'just touching' the circle at $(1,0)$. Notice that the tangent line doesn't change when you change the angle but its intersection with the secant line does.

The tangent line and the secant line form another (larger) triangle. Today we use $tan(a)$ to denote the height of this triangle and $sec(a)$ to denote the length of its hypotenuse.
